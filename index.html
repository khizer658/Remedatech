Global Health Challenges

Despite relentless progress in the field of medicine, every year, there are newly emerging infectious disease and pandemic threats and millions of deaths globally from noncommunicable diseases. Environmental factor such as climate change has major health implications. Air pollution is a major factor contributing to global climate crisis. Millions of deaths from chronic respiratory illnesses, cardiovascular disease, cancers, and diabetes are attributed to the same emissions responsible for global warming. Climate change also worsens malnutrition and promotes the spread of infectious diseases.

Our brain is the center of our body. The brain basically controls everything we do like walking, talking seeing, breathing, thinking, etc. That is why it is imperative to keep our brain in excellent working condition.

The foods we eat contain key nutrients, for example, B vitamins that play a role in keeping our brain healthy and improve specific neurological and mental functions, such as memory, learning, reasoning, thinking, planning, language, attention, balance, sensation, and a lot more.
Deficiency in these nutrients can lead to a myriad of health outcomes from anemia to a compromised immune system or neurological and
psychological dysfunction such as cognitive decline, persistent tingling in the hands and feet or problems with balance.
Certain groups, such as older adults and pregnant women, are more at risk for vitamin B deficiencies. This is not uncommon, as they need larger
amount of some types of vitamin B.

Malabsorption caused by infection, medications, small intestine surgery, and disorders such as celiac disease can lead to vitamin B deficiency as well.

Some individuals are deficient because they simply have not been consuming a balance meal with adequate supply.
